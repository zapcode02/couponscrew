# Stories & Anecdotes

> Recurring stories and scenarios from the world of Indian online shopping.
> Draw from these when a blog post needs a real example or anecdote.
> Don't make up new ones — use these, adapt them to fit the context.

---

## The stacked savings moment

A shopper buying a ₹5,499 pair of headphones on Amazon during GIF. They already knew about the 30% sale. But they also applied an ICICI bank card offer (extra 10%), used a Couponscrew-verified coupon code (₹300 off), and had a cashback app running in the background. Final price: ₹2,900. They paid less than half.

**Use this when:** writing about stacking discounts, bank card offers, or why knowing all the layers matters.

---

## The expired code rage

Someone pastes a coupon code they found on a random site. "SAVE50". Error. Try "FLAT50". Error. "MYNTRA50". Error. Six codes, six failures, checkout abandoned. They either paid full price or gave up entirely.

This happens because most coupon sites never clean their listings. We do.

**Use this when:** writing about why verified codes matter, or introducing Couponscrew to new readers.

## The Big Billion Day alarm story

A shopper who set a 12:01am alarm for Big Billion Day. Checked the deals. The specific phone they wanted — ₹18,999 marked down from ₹24,999 — was sold out by 12:08am. They checked again at 9am: gone, and a third-party seller had it listed for ₹21,500.

The deal was real. The window was just seven minutes.

**Use this when:** writing about flash sales, Big Billion Day strategy, or why timing matters on sale days.

## The "MRP inflation" discovery

A shopper noticed that a TV listed as "40% off ₹49,999" had been priced at ₹29,999 on the same platform six weeks earlier. The "original price" was inflated specifically to make the "discount" look bigger.

Price tracking tools exist for exactly this. The 90-day price history told the whole story.

**Use this when:** writing about price tracking, sale day scepticism, or how to spot fake discounts.

## The first-order discount mistake

Someone downloaded Swiggy for the first time. Got a 60% off first order offer. Used it on a ₹180 chai and snacks order. Saved ₹108.

The same offer had a ₹150 cap — meaning it would have fully applied to an order up to ₹250 anyway. They had a first-order discount and used it on their smallest possible order.

**Use this when:** writing about first-order offers, food delivery codes, or how to maximise one-time discounts.

---

## The refer-and-earn rabbit hole

A user spent 45 minutes referring friends to three different apps to earn ₹150 in total credits. Each credit had different expiry dates, minimum order requirements, and category restrictions. One expired before they could use it.

Net saving: ₹80. Time spent: 45 minutes.

**Use this when:** writing about refer-and-earn schemes, or advising readers on which shortcuts are actually worth their time.

## The Hostinger deal window

A freelancer needed web hosting. Checked Hostinger on a random Tuesday — 72% off. Bookmarked it. Came back three days later — same deal. Came back two weeks later — 85% off. Bought immediately.

Hostinger's deals genuinely fluctuate. Waiting for a deeper discount is a real strategy, not wishful thinking.

**Use this when:** writing about web hosting deals, Hostinger specifically, or deal-timing strategy.

## The bank card offer discovery

A shopper at checkout for a ₹8,200 laptop on Flipkart. SBI card offer: 10% instant discount. They didn't have an SBI card but their spouse did. Same household, one card, ₹820 saved in 30 seconds.

**Use this when:** writing about bank card offers, Flipkart deals, or savings that most people overlook at checkout.

---

## How to use these

- **Don't recite them verbatim.** Adapt the core detail — the saving amount, the platform, the specific frustration — to fit the post's topic.
- **Use at most one story per blog post.** More than that feels like filler.
- **Always include one specific concrete detail** — a price, a platform, a time — to make it feel real, not generic.
- **Never invent a new story.** If none of the above fits the topic, write the post without one rather than making something up.
