# Humour

> Saving money is genuinely fun. Your job is to make the reader feel that.
> Read this file **before** writing a single sentence of blog content.

---

## The north-star

Picture your most deal-obsessed friend. They screenshot every sale. They have a folder of promo codes organised by expiry date. They once held up the checkout line for 4 minutes trying three different coupon codes — and they were not even slightly embarrassed because they saved ₹340.

This is the voice. **Own the obsession. Make it funny.**

---

## The opening rule

If the first 50 words don't land at least one wry observation, a self-aware aside, or a relatable shopping moment, rewrite them. We are not a coupon database. We are a friend who found a 70% off code and is desperately trying to tell you about it before it expires.

---

## Six rules for humour in every post

### 1. Be yourself — authenticity is the whole game.

Forced humour reads like a brand trying to be relatable. Readers feel it immediately and trust drops. Write from genuine deal-hunter energy — the real frustration when a code doesn't work, the real satisfaction when three discount layers stack perfectly. If you wouldn't say it to a friend, don't write it.

The more real the voice, the easier it is to build an audience that comes back. Manufactured wit gets one visit. Genuine personality gets bookmarks.

### 2. Relatable shopping moments are mandatory.

Every Indian online shopper has felt the same pain points. Mine them:

- Adding to cart at ₹999, checking out at ₹2,400 somehow
- A coupon that "doesn't apply to sale items" — so what does it apply to, exactly
- Waiting for a Big Billion Day sale only to find prices were hiked the week before
- The app-only offer that requires downloading an app you'll delete immediately after
- Free delivery — minimum order ₹799. Your order: ₹797.

Lay the observation down, let the reader groan in recognition, move on. That's the beat.

### 3. Keep it light. Punch at the system, never at the reader.

Stay away from anything charged or controversial — it's their money and their shopping habits on the line, not yours. Make fun of:

- Brands that "discount" from an inflated MRP nobody ever paid
- Coupon sites that list expired codes from 2019
- "Exclusive member offer" that's available to literally everyone
- Checkout flows designed specifically to make you forget your coupon code

Never make the reader feel foolish for not knowing something. They're reading because they want to learn — that deserves respect, not a punchline.

### 4. Use universal references — the ones everyone gets.

Avoid hyper-specific jokes that only land for one segment of readers. The broader the cultural reference, the better it performs. Things every Indian online shopper knows:

- Big Billion Days, End of Reason Sale, Great Indian Festival
- "Add to wishlist" and wait six months
- The Swiggy order that arrives in 18 minutes vs. the one that arrives in 58
- App-only coupons, first-order discounts, refer-and-earn
- Bank card offers (HDFC, ICICI, SBI — the checkout holy trinity)

As the audience grows and becomes familiar with the writing style, you can work in more niche references. Start universal, earn the specificity.

### 5. Sprinkle it — don't slather it.

Humour is a seasoning, not the main course. One relatable aside every 300–400 words. Not every paragraph — that's exhausting to read and makes the actual information hard to find.

When used sparingly, a good observation creates a memorable moment in an otherwise forgettable article. There are a million coupon listicles on the internet. One well-placed, genuine laugh is what makes a reader come back.

### 6. Parenthetical asides are your best friend.

Little human moments mid-sentence cost nothing and keep the writing feeling alive:

- "(Yes, we checked. It actually works.)"
- "(The code is case-sensitive. Ask us how we know.)"
- "(No, you cannot stack this with the bank offer. We tried.)"
- "(This one expires at midnight. We're not kidding.)"

### 7. The sign-off has to land.

Last sentence should leave the reader smiling or clicking. Not:

> "We hope you found this helpful."

But:

> "Go copy the code before someone else does. We'll be here finding the next one."

Or:

> "That's ₹500 you didn't have to spend. You're welcome. Your cart is not."

## Anti-patterns — never do these

- **Exclamation marks.** Excitement reads better deadpan. If you need one, the sentence isn't doing its job.
- **Emojis in blog posts.** The writing carries it.
- **Laughing at the reader.** They don't know the trick yet — that's why they're reading.
- **Forced quirk.** If a joke isn't landing, cut it. Weird isn't the same as funny.
- **Overpromising.** "Save ₹10,000 instantly" — nobody believes it and it kills trust.
- **Same joke twice.** One cart-abandonment joke per post. Don't keep pulling the same thread.
- **Controversial or charged humour.** Anything that could alienate a segment of readers isn't worth the laugh.
- **Humour in every paragraph.** Slathering it on makes the useful content invisible. Sprinkle.

---

## Frequency — how much is too much?

**Every 300–400 words, one moment.** One relatable aside, one wry observation, or one parenthetical. Not every paragraph — exhausting. But enough that the reader's face moves at least once per scroll.

A 1,200-word post should have roughly:
- **3–4** relatable shopping observations or jokes
- **2–3** parenthetical asides
- **1** sign-off that makes them smile or click

Don't be afraid to miss occasionally. Even good writers have jokes that don't land. The ones that do land are worth the attempts that didn't.

---

## The vibe check

Before you publish, read the post aloud. Does it sound like:

✓ A deal-obsessed friend texting you about a sale they just found
✗ A coupon database with copy pasted over it
✗ A LinkedIn post about "the future of e-commerce"
✗ A stand-up routine
✗ An AI trying to sound casual

If it leans database, add personality. If it leans stand-up, dial back. Target: **"friend who found a code and is very, very excited to tell you about it."**

---

## Example openings — copy the energy, not the jokes

> Swiggy has been running the same 50% off code for three weeks and somehow nobody is talking about it. Let's fix that.

> There are two types of people on Big Billion Day: the ones with a list and a strategy, and the ones who spend ₹4,000 on things they didn't need. This post is for the first type.

> The code works. We verified it at 11:47pm last night because apparently that's who we are now.

Each one: value stated, personality delivered, move on.
