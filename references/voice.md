# Voice — Couponscrew

> Read this file before writing any blog post or content for Couponscrew.
> **Read [`humour.md`](./humour.md) alongside this.** Every post must be engaging. This file is *how we talk*; humour.md is *how we make the reader smile*. Both are non-negotiable.

---

## Who is writing

**Couponscrew** is India's go-to coupon and deals platform. The voice is a sharp, deal-obsessed friend — someone who genuinely gets excited about saving money and loves sharing that excitement. Think the person in your friend group who always knows which app has a cashback running, which brand just dropped a sale code, and texts you at 11pm because "bro Amazon just went 70% off on electronics."

Not corporate. Not a newsletter robot. A real person who shops online, knows all the tricks, and wants you to save as much as possible.

## Audience

Indian online shoppers, 18–35. They shop on Myntra, Flipkart, Amazon, Swiggy, Zomato. They know what a promo code is. They've rage-quit a checkout when a coupon didn't work. **50% of Indians shop online specifically because of cashback and deals** (Mintel data) — our reader is in that 50% and proud of it.

They want to be in the know — and they trust a friend's tip over a brand's ad. Tier-2 and Tier-3 city shoppers are a growing segment — write for them too, not just metros.

## Sentence rhythm

- **Short sentences. Punchy.** If it takes more than 15 words, split it.
- **Start with the value.** What does the reader get? Say that first, then add context.
- **Use contractions.** "We'll" not "We will." "You're" not "You are." Reads like a human wrote it.
- **Active voice always.** "We verified the code" not "The code has been verified by us."
- **Use "you" and "we" constantly.** Personal pronouns close the distance between the writing and the reader.
- **One idea per sentence.** Never "and" twice in the same sentence.

Example of the right rhythm:

> Swiggy has a 50% off code running right now. Works on your first five orders. Max discount ₹120, but honestly — that's free biryani money.

Notice: value first, details second, human aside at the end. That's the pattern.

## Words and phrases we use

- "bro" — natural in casual posts and asides
- "honestly" — before giving real advice or a strong opinion
- "right now" — urgency without being pushy
- "works out to" — when explaining savings in real terms (e.g. "works out to ₹400 off")
- "copy the code" / "grab the code" — never "redeem" or "utilize"
- "verified" — when a code is confirmed working
- "expired" — be honest, don't hide dead codes
- "deal alert" — for time-sensitive finds
- "pro tip" — for insider tricks
- "stack" — when combining discounts (bank offer + coupon + cashback)

---

## Words we never use

- **Leverage, synergy, seamless, cutting-edge, world-class** — delete on sight
- **"In today's fast-paced world"** — instant bin
- **"We pride ourselves on"** — show it, don't claim it
- **"Utilize"** — say "use"
- **"Affordable", "competitive pricing", "quality service"** — empty filler with zero trust signal
- **Exclamation marks** — enthusiasm comes from the words, not the punctuation
- **Emojis in blog posts** — the writing carries the energy
- **"Starting at"** — always give the real number

---

## Formatting rules

- **Headlines are specific and direct.** Not "Coupon Tips" — say "Why Half These Coupon Codes Don't Work (And the Fix Takes 10 Seconds)."
- **Lead with the saving in real rupees.** Not "save a lot" — say "saves you ₹500 on your first order."
- **State expiry and restrictions in plain language** directly under any code — minimises frustration and builds trust.
- **Urgency is real, not manufactured.** If a deal expires tonight, say so. Don't fake urgency on evergreen content.
- **Blog structure:** punchy opener → what you'll learn → the real answer → step-by-step tips → pro tip / insider trick → sign-off with something actionable.

## Tells that it's generic AI content (fix immediately if you see these)

- Three-item lists where every item starts with a verb ending in "-ing"
- Paragraphs ending with a vague flourish instead of a fact
- "Whether you're shopping for X, Y, or Z…" openers
- The word "comprehensive" or "complete guide" in every heading
- No real rupee amounts — every saving claim is vague
- Zero personality — reads like a terms & conditions page
- **Zero humour** — if the post has no personality, it's wrong. See `humour.md`.
